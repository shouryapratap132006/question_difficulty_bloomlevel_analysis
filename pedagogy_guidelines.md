# Pedagogical Guidelines & Best Practices for Question Design

## Bloom's Taxonomy Levels
1. **Remember**: Recalling facts and basic concepts. Define, duplicate, list, memorize, repeat, state.
2. **Understand**: Explaining ideas or concepts. Classify, describe, discuss, explain, identify, locate, recognize, report, select, translate.
3. **Apply**: Using information in new situations. Execute, implement, solve, use, demonstrate, interpret, operate, schedule, sketch.
4. **Analyze**: Drawing connections among ideas. Differentiate, organize, relate, compare, contrast, distinguish, examine, experiment, question, test.
5. **Evaluate**: Justifying a stand or decision. Appraise, argue, defend, judge, select, support, value, critique, weigh.
6. **Create**: Producing new or original work. Design, assemble, construct, conjecture, develop, formulate, author, investigate.

## Question Difficulty Factors
- **Cognitive Load**: How much working memory is required to answer the question. Multi-step problems increase load.
- **Language Complexity**: Vocabulary and sentence structure. Simple language reduces unnecessary confusion.
- **Distractors**: In multiple-choice, plausible distractors effectively distinguish between partial and full understanding.

## Learning Gap Remediation Strategy
- **Low Success Rate + Low Difficulty**: Usually indicates missing foundational knowledge. Recommendation: Review introductory concepts.
- **Low Success Rate + High Difficulty**: Expected, but if too low, the gap between instruction and assessment is too large. Recommendation: Provide scaffolding or intermediate practice.
- **High Success Rate + High Difficulty**: Indicates strong mastery or potentially a flawed question (e.g., obvious answer). Recommendation: Review question for unwanted hints.
- **High Time Taken**: Indicates either a complex, multi-step problem or confusing wording. Recommendation: Check for clarity if the question is meant to be simple.

## Actionable Recommendations for Educators
- **Scaffolding**: Break down complex questions into a series of simpler ones to guide student thinking.
- **Clarity**: Ensure the verb aligns with the intended Bloom's level. Do not use "Understand" if you mean "Apply".
- **Contextualization**: Connect abstract concepts to real-world scenarios to improve engagement and retention.
- **Formative Feedback**: Provide specific feedback on incorrect options to address common misconceptions.
